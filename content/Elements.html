Elements

Introduction

A tangram is a puzzle consisting of seven geometric shapes that can be arranged into a square. But they can also be arranged into hundreds of other silhouettes that look like animals, everyday objects, or other geometric shapes.

Tangrams are an illustration of the fundamental theory of chemistry. Just as the tangram is composed of seven pieces that are combined and re-arranged to form different shapes, matter is composed of atoms that combine and rearrange to form different substances. And just as the pieces of the tangram come in six different types, different types of atoms represent different elements.

Theories about elements and particles of matter have been around for a very long time. In 1661, Robert Boyle published a book called the Skyptical Chymist, almost 150 years before John Dalton shared his atomic theory in print in 1808. In it, he began with four propositions about matter. The language has been simplified and expressed in current terminology (the original can be found here for comparison), but these were Boyle’s assertions:

  1. 1.Matter is composed of particles of different sizes, shapes and motions. 

  2. 2.Particles of matter combine together to form molecules, which are the building blocks of the compounds we observe in nature. 

  3. 3.It should be possible by chemical means to decompose the molecules of compounds into their constituent substances. 

  4. 4.These constituent substances could rightly be called elements. 

The purpose of Boyle’s book was not to argue if elements existed, but to compare competing theories about how many elements existed and how they combined with each other.

But the concepts of elements and particles of matter are much older than even Boyle’s work. For example, salt can be extracted from sea water by pouring a bucket of sea water into a shallow pan and letting the heat of the sun evaporate the water. But now take the salt crystals left behind can be added to a glass of fresh water, and what was done has now been undone. It seems that sea water has at least two constituents which can be separated and recombined in a very simple fashion.

The concept of a particle of matter is also an old idea. Looking carefully at the salt deposited from seawater reveals a regular crystalline structure. Comparing salt crystals to a tiled floor with a geometric pattern immediately suggests a theory that crystals might be composed of particles ordered into a regular array.

So what took scientists so long to put atomic theory on a firm foundation, and to transform alchemy into chemistry?  There are at least three reasons.

First, chemical reactions can be complicated. Even the classic science project, reacting of vinegar and baking soda to make a ‘volcano’, is not quite as simple as A plus B makes C. Vinegar is a solution of acetic acid in water, and the carbon dioxide gas produced is the result of the decomposition of the carbonate ion. The full reaction is

HCH3CO2 + NaHCO3  CO2 + NaCH3CO2 + H2O

Understanding clearly what is happening on a chemical level is much easier when the reactions themselves are simple, but it is difficult to sort the complicated ones from the simple ones by observation alone.

Second, the chemical sciences needed a quantitative foundation. Alchemy was a ‘cookbook’ approach to chemistry, focusing on qualitative results. Many of the technologies to measure masses or volumes carefully, or the ability to isolate and control the process of a reaction, didn’t exist until the late 1700s. Antoine Lavoisier, a founder of modern chemistry, built his own balance which could measure mass to the closest 0.03 grams. And to prove his theory that water was not an element but a compound, he built an apparatus to show how it could be decomposed into hydrogen and oxygen, and then immediately re-synthesized into water.

Third, Dalton benefitted from the fact that many of his contemporaries were examining the same chemical phenomena as he was. Discussions among scientists about data and results are very important for the advancement of science, no matter what the field. Joseph Priestley, Humphrey Davy, Carl Scheele, and Jacques Charles were all conducting investigations into the nature of combustion and the behavior of gases around the same time as Dalton. Multiple observations, measurements and scientific conclusions coalesced into the supporting evidence for atomic theory.

The evidence for atomic theory collected during this time was ultimately summarized as four laws. The hypothetical data presented here is similar to the type that could have been collected by Dalton and his contemporaries. Once the laws of chemistry are established, the results can be used to build a reasonable and logical theory of chemical changes.

 

Combustion

In 1771, Carl Scheele investigated the combustion of many different substances. He and others recognized that air was necessary for the chemical change. A popular theory held that a burning substance lost something to the air, and that burning stopped when air had absorbed as much of the substance as it could. Lavoisier disproved the theory, demonstrating that the burning material gained something from the air, and that burning stopped when that substance was depleted. The substance gained by combustion is now known to be the element, oxygen.

Scheele was both a prolific and proficient chemist, and descriptions of his experiments in his own words can be found here. He included not only measures of amounts and volumes but also his methods for separating one type of gas from another and determining their quantities. Since it is not necessary to explain how the data is collected to understand how it leads to the theory of the atom, we will just accept the fact that it can be done and focus on the results.

 

Experiment 1: closed combustion

Suppose a sealable container has a volume around 10 liters and a mass of 132.54 g when completely evaluated. A 0.54 g sample of charcoal is placed in the container and the total mass when sealed is 145.40 g, meaning that 12.32 g of air is also in the vessel.

The vessel is heated until all of the charcoal is consumed. The pressure in the vessel is much higher, even after the container returns to its original temperature, meaning that more gas has been produced by combustion.

The container is weighed again after the reaction in complete, and the total mass is 145.40 g.

 

Conclusion #1:

The Law of Conservation of Mass.

 The mass of all the substances before a chemical change is equal to the mass of all the substances after a chemical change.

This law was discovered by Antoine Lavoisier is 1774.

 

Experiment 2: Separating products

The gas produced from the burning of charcoal is separated from the rest of the air. Its properties are the same as the substance Joseph Priestly reported on in 1774 that he called ‘fixed air’. The mass of the new gas is 1.98 grams.

Since not all of the air reacts with charcoal, we will assume that only part of it is involved in the reaction, the element now known as oxygen. This assumes that air is not a pure substance but a mixture of pure substances, which is confirmed by the fact that different portions of air liquefy at different temperatures.

Assuming that all the charcoal is contained in the gas, conservation of mass suggests the oxygen in the sample of ‘fixed air’ should be 1.98 - 0.54 =1.44 grams.

The experiment is repeated with twice the mass of charcoal, 1.08 grams, as the starting material. Results show that twice the amount of ‘fixed air’ is produced, 3.96 grams. Conclusion #2:

The Law of Constant Composition

The percent by mass of the elements is a pure substance is constant, no matter what the size or source of the substance.

This law was discovered by Joseph Proust is 1806.

 

Experiment 3: A different product

If the conditions of the combustion of charcoal (the element carbon) are now changed, the product is no longer ‘fixed air’ but a different substance with different properties. When the composition of the gas is analyzed, the percentages are 43% carbon and 57% oxygen with the ratio of oxygen to carbon at 1.33, exactly half the ratio for ‘fixed air’.

Here is another way to think about the result. Suppose 10.0 grams of carbon was combusted. If the product was ‘fixed air’, then 26.6 grams of oxygen would be consumed; but if the other gas was the product, then exactly half the amount of oxygen, 13.3 grams, is consumed.

This is not the only example of the same starting materials having two or more different products. Sulfur can combine with oxygen in different ways with one substance consuming 10 grams of oxygen for every 10 grams of sulfur burned, and the other requiring 15 grams of oxygen for every 10 grams of sulfur: a 3:2 ratio.

 

Conclusion #3:

The Law of Multiple Proportions

If two elements form more than one compound between them, then the ratios of the masses of the second element which combine with a fixed mass of the first element will be ratios of small whole numbers.

This law was discovered by John Dalton in 1808.

 

Reactions of Gasses

Solids are not the only substances that combine with oxygen. Gaseous hydrogen will also combine with it to form water.

Unlike solids and liquids, gases have very small densities and very large volumes. The volume of a gas can vary significantly based on both temperature and pressure, but at a particular temperature and pressure, the amount of a gas (its mass) is proportional to its volume. For example, one would expect that two liters of oxygen to have twice the mass as one liter at the same temperature and pressure.

When exploring the reactions of gases, a very interesting result was observed. Even though the picture shows not the formation but the decomposition of water from its two elements but the splitting of water into its elements, hydrogen and oxygen, clearly the volume of hydrogen is twice that of oxygen. Not only that, but when carried out as a formation reaction, exactly one volume of water vapor is formed from one volume of oxygen and two volumes of hydrogen.

 

This is not the only example of simple whole number ratios. Suppose sulfur is vaporized before combustion. Data would should that in one case, two liters of oxygen combine with one liter of sulfur vapor to form one liter of product; and in the other case, three liters of oxygen combine with one liter of sulfur vapor to form one liter of product.

 

Conclusion #4:

The Law of Combining Volumes

The volumes of the gaseous reactants and products at the same temperature and pressure can be expressed as ratios of small whole numbers.

This law was discovered by Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac in 1808.

 

Putting the laws together

Now that we know what is happening in a chemical reaction, it is time to develop a reasonable theory as to why it is happening. In 1811, Amadeo Avogadro made the following conjecture:

Equal volumes of gas at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of particles.

Avogadro’s theory was proposed to explain Gay-Lussac’s law of combining volumes and it was based on the assumptions of Dalton’s atomic theory.

From Avogadro’s conjecture, one would conclude that one liter of sulfur gas contains a certain number of sulfur particles, and two liters of oxygen gas contain exactly twice the number of particles. When the two elements react completely, one particle of sulfur combines with two particles of oxygen to form one particle of the product, which experimentally occupies one liter, as predicted by Avogadro. In modern notation and terminology, elemental sulfur and oxygen react to form sulfur dioxide, SO2.

But sulfur dioxide is not the only possible product when these elements react. Under different conditions, sulfur trioxide can form, SO3. Therefore, three liters of oxygen react with one liter of sulfur instead of two, which is also observed experimentally, with one liter of product formed.

But a greater volume of oxygen with a greater number of particles would have a greater mass as well. In fact, in the second case, 50% more oxygen – either by volume or mass – is need to form the product. This is exactly what is demonstrated by the sample data used to formulate Dalton’s law of multiple proportions: 10 grams of sulfur combines with 10 grams of oxygen in one case, but with 15 grams of sulfur in the second case.

Or, for the carbon-containing products, 10 grams of carbon combine with 13.3 grams of oxygen to form one product, or 26.6 grams (twice as much oxygen, or twice the number of oxygen atoms) to form ‘fixed air’. Therefore, the law of multiple proportions can be explained fully only if elements combine as whole particles.

There is only one piece left to this puzzle. If elements combine as whole-unit atoms, why are the mass ratios of the elements in a pure substance integers? In other words, if the ratio of carbon to oxygen is one-to-one, why don’t 10 grams of carbon combine with 10 grams of oxygen to form 20 grams of CO? This was perhaps the most important part of the theory proposed by Dalton in his 1808 paper. He suggested that different atoms had different relative masses.

The density of hydrogen gas at normal temperature and pressure is 0.090 g/L. That means 1.00 grams of hydrogen gas at this temperature and pressure would occupy 11.1 liters.

So what is the mass of 11.1 liters of oxygen gas under these conditions? Based on its known density, that volume of oxygen would have a mass of 16.0 grams. If Avogadro is correct, then each atom of oxygen is 16 times more massive than hydrogen.

Knowing the relative mass of oxygen compared to hydrogen is only the beginning. For example, 16 grams of oxygen will combine with 16 grams of sulfur to form sulfur dioxide. Since there is half as much sulfur compared to oxygen, sulfur must be twice as heavy, and the relative atomic mass of sulfur is experimentally determined to be 32.00, as shown on the periodic table.

There are two different combustion products of carbon with oxygen; the smaller ratio of oxygen to carbon is 1.33. Assuming a one-to-one ratio of carbon to oxygen (which turns out to be true – the product is carbon monoxide) then the relative mass of carbon must be three-fourths that of oxygen, or 12.00 relative mass units.

So in this fashion, either by reacting elements of unknown mass with those of known mass and deducing the simplest atom ratio, or by vaporizing elements and comparing the masses of equal volumes, a table of relative atomic masses can be built starting with hydrogen, the lightest element, having a relative mass around 1.00

As early as 1813 the Swedish chemist Berzelius experimentally measured the relative masses of 50 known elements to the greatest accuracy possible in his day. The complete current experimental results for atomic masses using much more accurate methods are summarized in a standard periodic table.

The Chemical Elements

Just as letters are the building blocks of words and words are the building blocks of the written language, elements are the building blocks of compounds and compounds are the building blocks of chemistry. And just as young children need to learn their ABC’s, it is important to have at least some knowledge of the common elements to make sense of the rest of chemistry.

It is not necessary to memorize all 118 element names and symbols, but here are 33 that are often encountered in familiar products or simple chemical reactions.

 

The Common Metals

Metals have the defining properties of being malleable, ductile, and electrically conducting. The common metals are relatively abundant and easy to obtain, often by smelting ores. They also tend to be stable in their natural state, although only a few of them such as copper, gold and silver can be found in nature as elements.

The Alkali Metals

The alkali metals have similar chemical properties. They react with water to form hydrogen gas and basic solutions; therefore, they don’t exist in their elemental state and are stored in an inert material such as oil. Sodium and potassium are often found in familiar chemical compounds such as salt, baking soda, and lye.

The Alkaline Metals

The alkaline metals have similar chemical properties and are not quite as reactive as the alkali metals. They also react with water to form hydrogen gas and basic solutions, but some such as magnesium and calcium can exist in their elemental forms although they do corrode. Magnesium and calcium are found in chemical compounds of milk of magnesia and chalk, respectively.
