Generalized Motion

 

Watch this short video of a mechanical toy.

 

 

The video demonstrates that interesting motions can appear to be very complicated. So how do we describe them?

 

In the study of motion, physicists have discovered that any complicated motion can always be subdivided into three mutually exclusive categories. Translation is moving from one point in space to another; it can occur in one, two or three dimensions. Rotation is motion around a fixed point. This motion is always two-dimensional and unlike translation, the object doesn’t actually ‘get’ anywhere. And an object doesn’t ‘get’ anywhere, either, with vibration. Instead of moving in circles, the object moves back-and-forth in a straight line about a fixed point.

 

For the purposes of this introduction to motion, we will only consider translational motion in one dimension, while vibrational motion will be explored in a another Unit. In a full physics course, projectile motion (a specific type of two-dimensional motion), circular motion, and rotational motion are also studied as separate topics.

The Elements of Motion

A scientist doesn’t want to just describe motion but to understand it and master it. That implies quantifying motion and its key elements.

 

The first fundamental element of motion is time. The experience of motion doesn’t make any sense without the passage of time. Time may be measured in minutes, hours, days or years, but for most applications the second is the standard unit of time.

 

The second element is location. The measurement of location requires an origin. The origin is completely arbitrary and determined by the investigator, although some origins are more useful than others. The Cartesian coordinate system is a useful method for describing the location of an object in a two-dimensional space. The distance of an object from the origin to its location can be measured with any unit of length, but the meter (or centimeter) is commonly used in laboratory situations. In this course, the English units of inches, feet or yards are NEVER used to measure distances!

 

The location of an object has two parts: a magnitude and a direction. There are so many quantities in physics that are described by both a magnitude and a direction that our general name for them is vectors. Quantities with magnitude only are scalars. Time is an example of a scalar quantity, since statements such as “ten seconds to the left” or “two weeks North” don’t make any sense. Location is a vector because “three steps forward” does make sense and means something very different than “three steps back”.

 

Direction is extremely important in the physical sciences but it isn’t often mentioned in early introductions to the natural sciences. However, anyone who has ever planned a trip or employed a GPS system knows that it makes a big difference turning left at a stop light than right, or traveling east instead of north. Or imagine following someone in a car moving thirty miles an hour, as opposed to meeting them at the same speed in a head-on collision. Direction makes a difference.

 


A vector quantity is often identified by two separate and independent measures but a scalar by only one. The vector quantity measuring location is displacement; its scalar counterpart is distance. The distance of the object from the origin in this figure is 5 meters. The displacement could be described by the (x, y) coordinate of (3, 4) [+3 meters in the x-direction and +4 meters in the y-direction], or as 5 meters at a 53 degree angle. The common system is to measure angles counter-clockwise with respect to the positive x-axis. Whether using x- and y-components or a magnitude/direction combination, two quantities are needed to uniquely specify the vector.


The actual motion of an object through space is described by the vector quantity of velocity. Its scalar counterpart is speed. Velocity is defined as a change in displacement over time; speed is a change in distance over time.


A fourth quantity of major importance in the study of motion is acceleration. Acceleration is speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction. Acceleration is defined as a change in velocity over time and it is always a vector. Negative acceleration is NOT the same thing as slowing down, because slowing down describes how the magnitude of the vector is changing, not its direction in space.

 

Representations of Motion

Because motion can be complicated and because position, velocity or acceleration can be different at each measured instance of time, physicists have developed methods to represent motion that have proven to be useful for describing and understanding the motion of objects. The one that probably comes to mind first is describing motion by equations. Although algebraic manipulation is a powerful tool in the study of motion, it should be the lastrepresentation method employed in the investigation of motion, not the first.

 

 

Beginning with Words and Pictorial Descriptions

 

Think of some words that can be used to describe the motion of the ladybug in the video. Spinning, turning, and flipping are some examples. The first step to working with a specific example of motion is to describe it in a sentence. The word description focuses the mind on what is important about the motion and identifies which of the three categories of motion apply to the situation at hand.

 

Since motion is about changing location in space, a pictorial description also makes a lot of sense. If there are two or more objects involved, then a drawing can show how they are situated in relation to each other. And if direction is important, arrows can be used to show the direction of velocity or acceleration.

 

 

Representing Movement in Motion Diagrams

 

A motion diagram is a series of pictures show the location of the object in equal time intervals. An arrow is attached to each picture to show the direction of the velocity at that point in time, with the length of the arrow representing the relative speed. An acceleration vector can also be attached to the picture or drawn near it. A motion diagram can represent either one-dimensional or two-dimensional motion.

 

 

Presenting Data in Graphs

 

The position, velocity, and acceleration of an object at particular times can be presented in a data table, but most people don’t process lists of data very well since most of us are visually oriented. However, that same information becomes understandable if it is plotted in graphs of position, velocity or acceleration as a function of time. A series of data points in a graph shows the sequence of position or changes in velocity or acceleration over time sequentially. The mind, through what the eye sees, has a better sense of how the object ‘moves’ in a graph, even though the graph is static and the object is not moving. Understanding in the sciences often requires imagination.

 

 

Summarizing Relationships in Equations

 

As mentioned in the unit on Scientific Methods, one of the foundational principles of the Newtonian philosophy is that algebra applied to equations describing an object or a system will lead to accurate predictions of behavior and results. But an equation is not just a set of letters and symbols separated by an equal sign. An equation is mathematical shorthand for a concepts. An equation has no meaning by itself: it is given meaning when those letters are linked to objects and their behaviors, and the symbols are linked to mathematical actions.

An equation is like a paragraph written in a foreign language: if you do know how to read the language, then a paragraph could be a translation of the Gettysburg address or Hamlet’s soliloquy. If you don’t know the language, then the passage is just a rectangle filled with letters and spaces.

 

Just as a word or pictorial description is the first step to examining a physical science problem concerning motion, thinking through the meaning of each letter as an equation is being written down is the first step to actually being able to use equations and algebra to any degree of success when using solving the problem mathematically.

 

 

 

Putting It All Together: An Example

Tony is dating Melissa. As he steps out the science building, he sees Melissa twenty meters ahead of him, walking toward the Student Union at a leisurely pace of one meter per second. He immediately beings to walk toward her at a constant acceleration of 0.5 m/s2. Where and when does Tony catch up to Melissa?

 

The problem already presents a word description of the motion. It includes useful information such as Melissa’s constant velocity (called uniform motion) and Tony’s constant acceleration. It provides the relative location of the two actors of this drama at the start of the clock and identifies the direction of motion.

 

The next figure is a pictorial representation on the motion. Since there are two objects, each with its own position, velocity, and acceleration,  a “T” or an “M” has been added to identify the phsical quantity for each person. The label “f” mean ‘final’, which is usually the last step when the problem is completed. An attempt has been made to include all the numerical information in the picture as well as clearly identify what we are looking for (when the problem is solved) with question marks.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The origin could have been either Tony’s or Melissa’s initial position, or some other arbitrary point. The final result of where they meet will be the same, but the numerical value associated with that location will be different depending on the origin: add twenty meters to it to find Tony’s walking distance if Melissa’s location is chosen as the origin; subtract twenty from it to find Melissa’s walking distance if Tony’s location is the origin. But the actual distances walked by Tony and Melissa do not depend on the choice of the origin.

 

Because we are interested in the distance Tony travelled, we will use his starting point as the origin. We have also decided that to the right will be the positive direction and to the left will be negative. Both Tony and Melissa are moving in a positive direction, although if we reversed the signs or direction of motion physics would be the same.

 

We can now create a motion diagram for this example. The steps for constructing one are given elsewhere, but the one drawn here shows that Tony is travelling greater distances as time progresses, and his speed is also increasing because the velocity vector arrow is getting longer. The text “v=0” is used to show that Tony started from rest. The arrow above Tony’s motion is his constant acceleration.

 

Melissa, on the other hand, has an acceleration of zero, so her pictures are equally spaced and the velocity vector arrow does not change in length or direction. Her acceleration is also specified as “a=0”. Notice also that we have included five time intervals for both Tony and Melissa since they are destined to meet at the same time.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

‘At the same time’ is an important verbal clue. It means their time of travel will be the same

 

tT = tM = t

 

and their final locations will be the same

 

dT,f = dM,f

 

These two important and necessary relationships are not found in a list of equations defining the relationships between position, velocity and acceleration, but are discovered by reading the problem carefully and interpreting what the phrases and sentences mean.

 

However the other equations needed to finish this problem do come from generalizations of the relationships between position, velocity and accelerations of objects moving with uniform motion (Melissa) or with constant acceleration (Tony).

 

dM = dM,0 + vMt

 

This equation means, “The position of Melissa at any later time can be found by taking her initial position when the clock starts and adding to it the product of her constant velocity and the elapsed time."

 

In a similar way, because Tony started his motion from rest,

 

dT = dT,0 + ½ a t2

 

which translated means, "Tony’s position at some later time is his initial position when the clock starts plus one-half the product of his constant acceleration and the square of the elapsed time."

 

The time of interest is the final time when Tony’s location matches Melissa’s so we drop the ‘f’ subscript. The variable ‘t’ is not subscripted because both Tony and Melissa and moving at the same time.

 

Now doing the algebra:

dM = dT

  dM,0 + vMt = dT,0 + ½ a t2

         20 + (1)t = 0 + (.5)(.5) t2

20 + t = .25 t2

t2 - 4t - 80 = 0                  

t =  +11.2 sec; t = -7.2 sec

 

A negative time doesn’t make physical sense so we use the 11.7 sec solution. With that we calculate Tony’s location as 31.2 meters from the origin, which by separate calculation is also Melissa’s location.

 

The graph of their positions over time shows that the negative time is the other point (in the past) where the two curves meet. But, obviously, the equations of motion do not correctly describe either Tony’s or Melissa’s motion before he stepped out the door of the science building, and therefore the second solution has no physical meaning.
 
This particular word problem is beyond the mathematics expected of students taking this course, but it does demonstrate the power of these representations of motion. It is how we sent a man to the Moon and Spirit and Opportunity to Mars. And understanding the physics of motion and using equations to describe it is how a gamer created Jump n Bump! to model realistic motion.
